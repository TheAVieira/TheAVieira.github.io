Turnkey project delivery - Single fiber wetting characterization

After my PhD I was invited by our collaborators to replicate the device and method I developed for measuring the wetting properties of single-fibers. I developed the method as a side-quest during my PhD. And that side-quest turned into a Best Conference Paper award at MARSS2023 and then into a serious application case. It so happened our collaborators were highly in need of an instrument capable of measuring the wetting properties of single-fiber. They are trying to modify the wetting properties of fibers and had no effective means of measuring them.

Hardware: I streamlined the hardware used in my device. Simplified the probe making it more robust and minimizing its micro-assembly procedure.

Software: I used the control software I rewrote from our group as control software (C#, Python, Lua). I wrote the device control code so the user would have comprehensive control over the device. I also rewrote the analysis software and made it more robust than before.

Documentation and User training: A significant part of the time was used to produce comprehensive documentation and train the team in using the device. Every usage aspect needed to be documented, e.g., device assembly and hardware, control software and usage protocols with examples of expected results.

 

Through drop-imaging | PhD

I invented and developed a class of novel robotic instrument for surface wetting characterization, at the Robotic Instruments group, Aalto University, Finland. The novelty lies in observing the wetting interface through the droplet itself which allows 40x better contact-angle resolution in super-repellent surfaces, compared to conventional methods.

In the process I also invented a new method to characterize advancing and receding contact angles on single-fibers. Turns out the "most obvious idea" had not been done before and required some engineering.

Hardware: I built an instrument to do it. It integrates precision motorized stages; machine vision cameras; a laser interferometer; a liquid dispenser and much more.

Software: I rewrote the mixed-language platform of our research group (C#, Python, Lua). This included writing low level code such as quadrature (sin/cos) decoding, real-time and offline image analysis in python and MATLAB.

Humidity control system

The goals was to control the Relative Humidity (RH) of any enclosure in our laboratory supporting a diverse set of experiments. To do this I started by considering the use of a CEM (Controlled Evaporator Mixer), including mass flow controllers for dry air and water. The CEM vaporizes the water into the dry air in precisely controlled ratio producing an output air at any RH. Turns out this would have been an over-engineered solution and quite expensive.

Instead I built a bubbler system. Dry air is bubbled through water to produce close to saturated air, ~80% RH. Higher RH levels can be achieved by heating the water. Then, two manual flow valves control the ratio of the flow of dry and humid air to be mixed. The system resulted to be quite repeatable and predictable. This means, you can calculate the final RH based on the ratio of dry and humid air and the same result is obtained each time. temperature and RH sensor is also used to monitor the state of the enclosure. I was very happy with the acrylic support structure that makes the device portable. It was design in CAD laser-cut to order. The water bottle is also surprisingly secure in place.

Winter lighting solution

The goal was to create a lighting solution that would light-up at dawn and dusk in winter time, replacing the missing hours of sun. However, it is not that simple. Here is how the device works:

Dawn cycle: In a winter day, at some prescribed hour at dawn, the lights dim up slowly, emulating a sunrise. The lights start with warm color and turn cold as the hours pass. Then the lights fade out when the actual sun comes up.
Dusk cycle: At some prescribed hour at dusk, the lights dim up, replacing the setting sun. The lights start with cold color. Then, as the hours pass, the lights turn warm and slowly fade out emulating a sunset.

To make this project I bought the LED strips and mounted them to aluminum profile bars atop my window hangers. For the control circuit, I needed a microcontroller with WiFi capability to synchronize the internal watch with a reference time. So I used an ESP8266MOD I had in my electronics bins. I did a bunch of research on daylight hours and made several calculations. The device was ready at the end of summer of 2020, some two weeks before the date when it would first turn on (i.e. daylight hours were low enough). So, I put the project on pause for two weeks. When the time came, I plugged the device and started testing. I made the device work with basic functionality but realized that to test it I would need to either collect data on device operation over a whole year or develop a testing methodology to simulate different days of the year and times of day.
In the end, I left the device work for a few days to see how I felt with the light coming on at the prescribed hours. But soon enough the AMS1117 LDO voltage regulator that powered the ESP8266MOD burned out. I suspect that the soldering process made the regulator bad. Puzzle Box

A simple 3D printed puzzle box. The challenge is to open the box. My goal was to design an elegant puzzle-box with focus on aesthetics, especially surface finish. Nonetheless, the design of the locking mechanism had to be optimized, including steel parts and magnets. I must have made around 20 prototypes and produced around 10 units, most of them gifted to friends. Many hours were spent on sanding, polishing, painting and airbrushing.

The piece is minimalist. The symmetric shape of box is broken by a fine white line that helps keep the user oriented. On one of the sides there is some alien-looking text that further breaks symmetry. The text instructs on how to open the box. If you can read it. The puzzle is considered solved when you have opened it and have deciphered the text. On the inside you'll find my maker's logo, which are my initials 'AV' written with same characters. Size: 50 x 50 x 23 mm.

For CAD design I typically work with SolidWorks, but for this project I wanted to try something different, so I went with Fusion 360. The parts are printed on an Elegoo Mars resin printer, then assembled and tested manually followed by surface finish.
